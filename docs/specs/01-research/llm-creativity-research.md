## Can LLMs Truly Be Creative? Experimental Evidence and Benchmarks

**Defining “Creativity”** – In human terms, creativity is often defined as the ability to produce work that is **novel**, **surprising**, and **valuable**[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2304.00008v5#:~:text=Margaret%20Boden%20defines%20creativity%20as,In%20other). Margaret Boden’s framework distinguishes *combinatorial creativity* (new combinations of familiar ideas), *exploratory creativity* (exploring conceptual spaces under fixed rules), and *transformational creativity* (changing the rules to generate the truly unprecedented)[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2304.00008v5#:~:text=match%20at%20L505%20be%20deemed,Then%2C%20we%20have%20examined). The question is whether large language models (LLMs) can meet these criteria or only imitate creativity. Due to their training on vast text corpora, LLMs certainly *appear* creative – generating poems, stories, jokes, etc. that can surprise or entertain. However, assessing “true” creativity requires rigorous tests.

**Creativity Benchmarks for LLMs** – Recent research has adapted human creativity tests to systematically measure LLM performance. Common benchmarks include **divergent thinking tasks** like the *Alternative Uses Task (AUT)* (list as many creative uses for an object as possible) and the *Divergent Association Task (DAT)* (list unrelated words to gauge associative originality)[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.12320#:~:text=have%20become%20more%20creative%20over,of%20human%20creativity)[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2501.19361#:~:text=Creativity%20tests%20for%20humans,46%29%20%28FF%29%2C%20measures). Creative story writing is another test domain, evaluated via expert or crowd ratings and metrics (novelty, surprise, coherence, etc.). For example, Chakrabarty *et al.* (2024) introduced the **Torrance Test of Creative Writing (TTCW)** – 14 binary criteria (fluency, flexibility, originality, elaboration) adapted from the Torrance Tests of Creative Thinking[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.14556#:~:text=Torrance%20Test%20of%20Creative%20Thinking,automate%20the%20TTCW%20evaluation%2C%20revealing). Such benchmarks provide quantitative “creativity scores” for LLM outputs, enabling comparison to human performance.

**LLMs vs Humans on Creativity** – Empirical results paint a nuanced picture. On one hand, **state-of-the-art LLMs have achieved human-level performance on certain creativity tests**. Multiple studies in 2023–2024 found models like ChatGPT (GPT-3.5/4) score *on par with average humans* in divergent thinking tasks[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2501.19361#:~:text=comparing%20LLM%20and%20human%20creativity,62). In fact, Haase *et al.* (2025) tested 14 models (GPT-4, Anthropic Claude, Llama, etc.) on AUT and DAT, and *all models* scored **above the human average** on the AUT[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.12320#:~:text=%28AUT%29,of%20human%20creativity). In the same study, GPT-4 (and a newer OpenAI model “o3-mini”) achieved the highest creativity scores, outperforming other LLMs[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.12320#:~:text=%28AUT%29,of%20human%20creativity). Bellemare-Pepin *et al.* (2024) even report evidence that **LLMs can *surpass* human capabilities** on specific creative tasks like divergent association and creative writing[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.13012#:~:text=creativity%20in%20both%20state,that%20can%20be%20artificially%20generated). These results suggest LLMs are not just regurgitating seen examples – they can generate genuinely novel ideas by human standards.

On the other hand, LLMs still **struggle to match the very best of human creativity**. Haase *et al.* found that although average performance was high, only **0.28%** of LLM outputs reached the **top 10%** of human creativity benchmarks[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.12320#:~:text=previous%20studies,model). In other words, it’s rare for a model to produce something as original as a top human expert’s answer. Similarly, in open-ended **short story generation**, Orwig *et al.* (2024) observed no significant difference between the *average* creativity ratings of human vs. ChatGPT stories (non-expert judges often rated them comparably)[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02316v4#:~:text=other%20hand%2C%20Orwig%20et%20al,collection%20of%20different%20models%20and). But a deeper analysis by Akoury *et al.* (2024) found that **model-written stories, while fluent, were less novel, less surprising, and more stereotyped than human-written stories**[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02316v4#:~:text=performance%20based%20on%20multiple%20dimensions,compared%20to%20average%20human%20writers). The models tended to use more complex wording (impressing lay judges), yet **lacked originality and diversity** compared to human storytellers[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02316v4#:~:text=performance%20based%20on%20multiple%20dimensions,compared%20to%20average%20human%20writers)[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02316v4#:~:text=Additionally%2C%20we%20collect%20fine,implications%20of%20our%20work%20for). This indicates that naïve evaluations might overestimate LLM creativity – expert assessment reveals that models lean on safe, familiar patterns (complex vocabulary, common tropes) rather than truly inventive narratives.

Crucially, LLMs falter when compared to **highly creative human experts**. In the TTCW evaluation, **professional authors’** short stories passed 3–10× more creative criteria than stories generated by GPT-style models[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.14556#:~:text=Test%20of%20Creative%20Writing%20,correlate%20with%20the%20expert%20assessments). LLM outputs often failed tests of originality and elaboration that human writers aced. Moreover, the AI stories frequently relied on clichés or predictable, “overly moralistic” endings[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2409.16605#:~:text=creativity,of%20widely%20accepted%20standards%20for), as noted by Chakrabarty *et al.* (2023). In creative writing, models have a tendency to produce pastiche or even **plagiarism** (verbatim training data) when pushed beyond their usual distribution[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.00492#:~:text=tasks%2C%20and%20their%20outputs%20vary,this%20claim%20using%20a%20narrative) – a clear limitation on true creativity.

**Summary of Findings:** Today’s general-purpose LLMs **do exhibit a degree of creativity** – they can generate novel and useful ideas and often meet or exceed *average* human performance on standardized creative tasks[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.12320#:~:text=%28AUT%29,of%20human%20creativity). This is strong evidence that LLMs can recombine knowledge in creative ways. However, the **“ceiling” of their creativity is lower than humans at their most creative**. Models predominantly excel at *combinatorial creativity* (mashing up existing concepts) and mild *exploratory creativity*, but they **rarely achieve transformational creativity** that breaks fundamental new ground[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2304.00008v5#:~:text=be%20deemed%20as%20creative%3B%20we,Then%2C%20we%20have%20examined). In practice, an LLM might produce 10 story ideas that *seem* original, yet an expert might recognize most as variations on common themes. Thus, while LLMs can *emulate* creativity to a remarkable extent (even surprising their own creators at times), researchers caution that they are **not creative in the open-ended, autonomous way humans are** – at least not yet[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2304.00008v5#:~:text=be%20deemed%20as%20creative%3B%20we,Then%2C%20we%20have%20examined). The next sections discuss how we can *coax more creativity* from LLMs, which models and settings work best, and what limitations persist.

## Prompt Engineering Techniques to Enhance LLM Creativity

Careful **prompt engineering** is critical to elicit the most creative outputs from an LLM. Since these models respond to the input text’s cues, phrasing the prompt in ways that encourage originality and imaginative responses can make a significant difference. Recent studies and best practices highlight several prompt strategies for boosting creativity:

- **Explicit Instructions for Creativity:** Simply asking the model to “be creative” or “think outside the box” can have a measurable (if modest) effect. For instance, Wenger & Kenett (2023) found that **altering the system prompt to explicitly encourage higher creativity** *slightly* increased an LLM’s originality scores and diversity of responses[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2501.19361#:~:text=Altering%20the%20LLM%20system%20prompt,responses%20are%20still%20more%20variable)[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2501.19361#:~:text=Table%207,lower%20than%20that%20of%20humans). For example, instead of a neutral system message (“You are a helpful assistant.”), one can set a creative tone: *“You are a creative storyteller who always finds novel, surprising angles.”* Such direct instructions nudge the model toward more adventurous completions. Empirically, the gains from this tactic are incremental – one study notes it “slightly increases individual creativity” but does not fully overcome the model’s inherent output homogeneity[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2501.19361#:~:text=more%20creative%20system%20prompts%20slightly,lower%20than%20that%20of%20humans). Still, it’s a **low-effort improvement** that developers should utilize. Always **clarify in the prompt** that you want imaginative, original answers (e.g. “Give an unusual and original solution…”).
- **Role Prompting (Persona Adoption):** Having the LLM *pretend to be* someone or something with a creative persona can unlock more inventive responses. Research by Lu *et al.* (2024) demonstrated that assigning distinct **roles** to the model (e.g. “a poet,” “a comedian,” “a scientist,” etc. in a panel discussion) helps combat the default “single style” of the LLM and yields more diverse ideas[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2405.06373v1#:~:text=responses%20to%20open,Test%20through%20both%20LLM%20evaluation). The prompt might say: *“Imagine you are a fantasy novelist and I am your editor. Write a fantastical story idea that no one has seen before.”* By role-playing, the model taps into different aspects of its training (e.g. style of a poet vs. a scientist) and produces content with those varied perspectives[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2405.06373v1#:~:text=discussions%20with%20participants%20from%20diverse,frameworks%20across%20various%20creativity%20metrics). This technique essentially **broadens the model’s output distribution** by leveraging its ability to mimic many voices. **Example Prompt (Role):** *“You are Dr. Seuss mixed with Shakespeare. In the voice of a whimsical poet, invent a new metaphor for time.”* – This prompt combines personae to encourage a very novel metaphor, pushing the model beyond a plain answer.
- **Few-Shot Priming with Creative Examples:** Providing the model with a few examples of creative outputs in the prompt (i.e. in-context learning) can prime it to continue in that vein. For instance, you might show two QA pairs where the answers are highly imaginative or humorous, and then ask the model to answer a new question. This technique leverages pattern learning – the model will attempt to match the creative style illustrated. While formal literature on few-shot prompt *specifically* for creativity is sparse, it follows from the general success of in-context learning. **Caution:** Make sure example responses truly exhibit the creativity you want; otherwise the model will mirror a generic style. Also, avoid over-constraining – too many examples might reduce originality by forcing the model to stick closely to them.
- **Divergent Thinking Prompts (Brainstorming):** Instead of asking for a single answer, prompt the model to generate **multiple ideas or variations**. This is akin to brainstorming and encourages divergent thinking. For example: *“List five original alternatives to the classic fairy tale ending ‘...and they lived happily ever after.’”* or *“Give me three wildly different ideas for a science fiction story about space travel.”* By explicitly requesting **multiple outputs in one go**, the model is pushed to explore more of the possibility space, often yielding at least one or two very creative options. Researchers have noted that LLMs show substantial **intra-model variability** – the same model can produce both mundane and highly original answers on different attempts[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.12320#:~:text=only%200.28%25%20of%20LLM,model%20selection%2C%20prompt%20design%2C%20and). Taking advantage of this by generating many candidates in parallel (or in one prompt) increases the chance of hitting upon a gem. In practice, users and developers find that *asking for N ideas and then picking the best* combines quantity with quality. This strategy is backed by Haase *et al.*’s finding that a **single prompt can have outcomes ranging from “below-average to original” creativity**[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.12320#:~:text=only%200.28%25%20of%20LLM,model%20selection%2C%20prompt%20design%2C%20and) – so it’s wise to get a spread of outputs and not rely on one shot.
- **Open-Ended, Rich Contexts:** How a task is framed can influence creativity. Prompts that set a vivid scene or give an intriguing, open-ended scenario tend to invoke more creative detail from an LLM. For example: *“The year is 2085, and sentient plants have taken over cities. Describe an invention a human might create to peacefully coexist with these plants.”* – This prompt provides a specific but imaginative context that invites a creative solution. In contrast, a dry prompt (“How can humans live with intelligent plants?”) might yield a more generic answer. While this is more art than science, **prompt phrasing** that includes unusual or evocative details will often lead the model to mirror that creativity in its answer.

**Critical Analysis:** Prompt engineering is a powerful lever, but it has limitations. Explicit creativity instructions yield only modest improvements[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2501.19361#:~:text=more%20creative%20system%20prompts%20slightly,lower%20than%20that%20of%20humans) – the model might still default to safe answers with slightly more descriptive language. Role prompting and priming can guide style, yet the **fundamental originality** of content is bounded by what the model “knows.” If an LLM’s training data lacks a truly novel idea, no prompt can magically synthesize pure innovation beyond that data (at best it will interpolate or combine known ideas in new ways). Additionally, complex prompts (with multiple examples or elaborate role setups) can make the model *more likely to misinterpret* or get constrained in a certain format. It’s important to test and iterate on prompts. Mohammadi (2024) emphasizes that with highly capable base models, **skilled prompt engineering is needed to harness their full creative potential without heavy-handed alignment**[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.05587#:~:text=diversity,creative%20potential%20of%20base%20models). In summary, good prompts **can coax significantly more creative output**, but they are not a guarantee – one may still need to generate multiple attempts and refine the prompt iteratively.

## Which LLMs Are Best Suited for Creative Tasks?

Not all language models are equal when it comes to creativity. Generally, **larger, state-of-the-art models** with broad training (so-called general-purpose foundation models) perform best on creative tasks. They have ingested diverse styles and domain knowledge, enabling more varied and contextually rich generation. Based on recent evaluations:

- **GPT-4 and GPT-3.5** (OpenAI) – *Closed models, top performers:* GPT-4 (2023) is often cited as **one of the most creative general models** available. In formal tests, GPT-4 has consistently scored at or near the top on creative benchmarks like AUT[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.12320#:~:text=%28AUT%29,of%20human%20creativity) and story generation tasks[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02316v4#:~:text=other%20hand%2C%20Orwig%20et%20al,collection%20of%20different%20models%20and). Its generations tend to be coherent and detailed, which helps with complex creative tasks (long narratives, in-depth metaphors, etc.). OpenAI’s earlier GPT-3.5 (as used in ChatGPT) is also quite capable, but studies indicate it is more prone to generic or repetitive outputs compared to GPT-4 on tasks like storytelling[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02316v4#:~:text=performance%20based%20on%20multiple%20dimensions,compared%20to%20average%20human%20writers). GPT-4’s advantage likely comes from its larger size and fine-tuning, giving it a better “creative repertoire.” In a 2024 study, **writers who used GPT-4 as a co-creator produced more creative stories than those writing alone**[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2501.19361#:~:text=example%2C%C2%A0Doshi%20and%20Hauser%20,This%20phenomenon%20of) – evidence of GPT-4’s strength as a creative aid (though those AI-aided stories ended up more similar to each other, a point we revisit later). OpenAI has also introduced **“o3” series models** (e.g. *o3-mini*, Jan 2025) that are smaller but optimized; one surprise finding was that a 6B-parameter o3-mini model achieved creativity scores on AUT comparable to GPT-4[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.12320#:~:text=%28AUT%29,of%20human%20creativity). This suggests rapid progress: even compact models, when expertly fine-tuned, can rival the largest models on divergent thinking tasks.
- **Anthropic Claude** – *Aligning creativity and safety:* Claude 1 and 2 from Anthropic (2023) are instruct-tuned LLMs comparable to OpenAI’s models. They are known for producing **helpful, less toxic outputs**, but how do they fare in creativity? The 2025 multi-LLM study by Haase *et al.* included Claude among 14 models and found Claude performed **above human average on AUT**[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.12320#:~:text=%28AUT%29,of%20human%20creativity), though not at GPT-4’s very top level. Claude’s strengths are in maintaining coherence and following instructions (due to its alignment training), which can actually prevent it from taking wild creative risks. So, Claude tends to give *sensible* creative answers (good for business applications of creativity, like marketing copy), but might not be as unconstrained or “crazy” as GPT-4 can be when prompted for something absurd. Still, Claude is regarded as one of the **best general models for creative brainstorming** in practical use, often producing imaginative and empathetic prose.
- **Google’s PaLM 2 and Gemini** – *Up-and-coming contenders:* Google’s PaLM 2 (used in Bard, 2023) has a huge training corpus and demonstrated ability in tasks like coding and logic. For open-ended creative tasks, PaLM 2/Bard can generate stories and jokes reasonably well, but academic benchmarks specifically on creativity are limited (since these models are not as openly evaluated on arXiv). Early reports and user studies suggest Bard’s creativity is decent but slightly less consistent than GPT-4’s – sometimes clever, other times bland. Meanwhile, Google’s next-gen model **Gemini** (early versions tested in late 2024) is expected to excel in creative tasks, combining language and multimodal capabilities. One study included a *“Google Gemini 1.5”* model in a creativity diversity test[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2501.19361#:~:text=match%20at%20L323%20large%202407%2C,all%20Llamas%2C%20all%20GPTs); while results weren’t highlighted model-by-model, the fact that researchers are testing Gemini indicates it’s considered a strong general-purpose model for the future. As of 2025, however, publicly *documented* evidence still places GPT-4 at the cutting edge for most creative benchmarks.
- **Open-Source LLMs (LLaMA, Mistral, etc.)** – *Closing the gap, with fine-tuning:* Open models like Meta’s **LLaMA-2** (2023) and the newer **Mistral 7B/17B** (2023) are widely available and have been fine-tuned into various chat models (Alpaca, Vicuna, etc.). Out-of-the-box, these base models are less aligned and sometimes more willing to produce unusual outputs (which can be good for creativity, but also risky). Fine-tuned versions (for helpfulness) may lose some spontaneity. Nguyen *et al.* (2024) note that **diversity can drop in RLHF-tuned models**[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.05587#:~:text=reduce%20these%20issues%2C%20their%20impact,and%20creativity%20in%20aligned%20models), which applies to many open-source chat models. Nonetheless, when specifically fine-tuned for creative tasks or using advanced decoding (see next section), even smaller open models can perform impressively. For example, Mistral and a research Llama-3 were used to test a new sampling method, yielding creative writing quality that humans preferred[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.01082#:~:text=experiments%20on%20benchmarks%20including%20GPQA%2C,on%20improving%20text%20generation%20quality). In practice, an open model ~13B–70B with the right prompting can generate very creative content – just perhaps with a bit more editing needed. The advantage is that open models can be further trained on **creative writing datasets** (e.g. fine-tune on fiction or humor corpora) to enhance their creative strengths beyond what general training gives.

**Bottom Line:** As of 2025, **the most capable general-purpose LLMs (GPT-4, Claude 2, etc.) are the top choices for creative tasks** – they combine a vast knowledge base with coherent text generation, which helps produce imaginative yet readable outputs. They tend to outperform smaller or older models in human evaluations of creativity[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.12320#:~:text=%28AUT%29,of%20human%20creativity). However, the gap is narrowing as new models and fine-tuning approaches emerge. In some studies, a fine-tuned smaller model can rival a giant model on creativity tests[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.12320#:~:text=%28AUT%29,of%20human%20creativity), which is encouraging for those who need local or cheaper solutions. It’s also worth noting that **model diversity matters**: If possible, one might use multiple models in tandem (to get different “perspectives”) because even the best model can get stuck in certain modes. Indeed, a study found that different LLMs often produce *very similar* outputs to each other (homogeneity issue)[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2501.19361#:~:text=responses%20from%20humans%20and%20a,limited%20set%20of%20%E2%80%9Ccreative%E2%80%9D%20outputs), so “best” might mean using a mix. But if one must pick a single model today for a creativity-intensive application, a top-tier model like GPT-4 is the safest bet for quality, and an open-source large model fine-tuned on creative content is the next best option.

## Decoding Settings and Model Configurations for Maximized Creativity

Choosing the right **generation settings (decoding parameters)** is just as important as the model and prompt for eliciting creative results. Two key parameters are **temperature** and **nucleus sampling (top-p)**, which control randomness and diversity in the output. There is often a trade-off between *creativity* and *coherence/quality*, so tuning these parameters is about finding a sweet spot. Scientific studies have examined how these settings affect measurable creativity:

- **Temperature** – This parameter (usually between 0 and 1, but can exceed 1 for extreme randomness) scales the probability distribution of next words. Higher temperature makes the model more likely to pick less probable words, leading to more surprising or diverse outputs. It’s commonly called the “creativity knob.” *Is higher always better for creativity?* **Not exactly.** Peeperkorn *et al.* (2024) rigorously tested a narrative generation task across temperature settings[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.00492#:~:text=generation%20task%20with%20a%20predetermined,slightly%20more%20novel%20outputs%20as). They found that as temperature increases from 0 to 1, **novelty of the text does increase slightly** – the outputs contain more unique ideas or phrases[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.00492#:~:text=temperature%20values%20using%20four%20necessary,chance%20via%20changing%20the%20temperature). However, the correlation was weak: going from temp 0.7 to 1.0 might yield only a modest uptick in originality. Meanwhile, **incoherence rises much more sharply** at high temperatures[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.00492#:~:text=temperature%20values%20using%20four%20necessary,chance%20via%20changing%20the%20temperature). Models start producing disjointed or off-topic sentences once randomness passes a certain threshold. Interestingly, they observed no significant change in “typicality” (whether the output is on-topic) with temperature[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.00492#:~:text=temperature%20values%20using%20four%20necessary,on%20creativity%20is%20far%20more) – so you get more gibberish but not necessarily more *meaningful* creativity. The conclusion was that *temperature alone is a blunt instrument*: it provides **some more creativity “juice,” but also more chaos**[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.00492#:~:text=temperature%20is%20weakly%20correlated%20with,via%20changing%20the%20temperature%20parameter). Thus, treating temperature as the sole “creativity parameter” is misleading – it needs to be balanced with other controls. In practice, many users find a temperature around *0.7–1.0* is good for creative tasks. Values near 0 (deterministic or beam search) produce dull, repetitive text (the model always picks the highest-probability, most “expected” completion, which often lacks imagination). Very high values (>1.2) can produce nonsense. So, **moderate-to-high temperature** is generally recommended to allow divergent ideas, but beware that beyond a point you get diminishing returns in creativity and a steep drop in coherence[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.00492#:~:text=temperature%20values%20using%20four%20necessary,chance%20via%20changing%20the%20temperature). It’s often best to try a few values (e.g. 0.7 vs 0.9) and evaluate output quality.
- **Top-p (Nucleus) Sampling** – Instead of (or in addition to) temperature, nucleus sampling limits the model to considering only the top *p* fraction of cumulative probability mass at each step[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.01082#:~:text=from%20a%20probability%20distribution%20over,1B). For example, top-p = 0.9 means only tokens from the most likely 90% of the distribution are eligible, cutting off the tail of very unlikely tokens. This ensures a baseline of coherence by avoiding ultra-rare words. Using nucleus sampling *prevents* the model from wandering into extremely low-probability (and often irrelevant) words even if temperature is high. It was introduced to improve the quality of random sampling by *“keeping the fun, removing the nonsense.”* The standard setting for creative tasks is usually **top-p around 0.8–0.95**, which allows plenty of diversity but prunes truly bizarre completions. If top-p is too low (e.g. 0.3), outputs become conservative and repetitive (only very likely words each time). If top-p = 1 (no cutoff), then the full effect of temperature comes through, including potential incoherence. Researchers have noted that **top-p can struggle at high temperatures** – when the model’s distribution is very flat (due to high temp), even the top 90% may include odd choices[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.01082#:~:text=from%20a%20probability%20distribution%20over,1B). New methods aim to improve on top-p’s balance.
- **Advanced Sampling Methods:** In late 2024, Nguyen *et al.* proposed **min-p sampling** as an enhancement to nucleus sampling[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.01082#:~:text=quality%20and%20diversity%2C%20especially%20at,source%20LLM). Instead of a fixed probability cutoff, *min-p sampling dynamically adjusts the cutoff based on the model’s confidence*. Essentially, it scales the threshold relative to the top token’s probability at each step[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.01082#:~:text=quality%20and%20diversity%2C%20especially%20at,in%20both%20text%20quality%20and). This means if the model is uncertain (flat distribution), it still allows more diversity, but if the model is confident in a particular continuation, it doesn’t force inclusion of a bunch of low-probability options. The result was an **improvement in both quality and diversity** of generated text, *especially at higher temperatures*[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.01082#:~:text=experiments%20on%20benchmarks%20including%20GPQA%2C,on%20improving%20text%20generation%20quality). In creative writing benchmarks, min-p sampling outputs were preferred by human evaluators for being more interesting *without* becoming incoherent[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.01082#:~:text=experiments%20on%20benchmarks%20including%20GPQA%2C,on%20improving%20text%20generation%20quality). This method has been adopted in some open-source libraries now. Similarly, other decoding techniques like **“typical sampling”** have been explored (which tries to maintain the entropy of the distribution near a target value). The goal of all these is to get more *controlled creativity*: not just relying on random chance (temperature) but steering generation to be novel yet relevant.
- **Other configuration tips:** A high-level setting is **model length and context** – enabling the model to generate longer outputs or giving it a longer prompt context can foster creativity (more room to develop ideas). However, extremely long generations can drift off-topic. Sometimes imposing a constraint or style via the prompt can actually boost creative thinking (paradoxically, constraints can inspire creativity). For example, instructing the model to answer in rhyme or in only 50 words can lead to imaginative solutions as the model works around the constraint. This isn’t a library parameter but a prompt choice. Another “configuration” strategy is **multi-pass decoding**: run the model N times and use a secondary process to select the best or combine them. This isn’t a single-run setting but is highly effective; it’s essentially manual *self-consistency*. Given that the **same model + prompt can yield varied results**[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.12320#:~:text=only%200.28%25%20of%20LLM,need%20for%20more%20nuanced%20evaluation), generating say 5 candidates and picking the most original (either by human judgment or using a learned creativity scorer[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2412.06060v1#:~:text=We%20construct%20an%20estimator%20to,judgment%2C%20unlike%20simple%20prompting%20baselines)) can dramatically improve outcomes. Some research even tries to automate this by having the model *score its own outputs* for creativity. For instance, Lee *et al.* (2024) found a latent “creativity” direction in a model’s activation space and used it to rate generations[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2412.06060v1#:~:text=We%20apply%20our%20creative%20steering,and%20creative%20than%20baseline%20generations)[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2412.06060v1#:~:text=similarity%20to%20the%20creative%20attribute,8B%20model%20to%20be%20a). While the base model alone was a poor judge of its creativity, techniques like this hint at future workflows: generate many, then filter by an internal creative heuristic.

In practice, to **maximize creativity**, one might use **temperature ~0.8–1.0 with nucleus sampling ~0.9**, possibly augmented by an advanced method like min-p. Then, allow the model to produce several outputs and either manually or automatically pick the most creative. Empirical data supports this approach: higher sampling randomness yields more novel ideas (up to a point)[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.00492#:~:text=temperature%20values%20using%20four%20necessary,chance%20via%20changing%20the%20temperature), and human evaluators prefer the results when diversity is boosted in a controlled way[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.01082#:~:text=experiments%20on%20benchmarks%20including%20GPQA%2C,on%20improving%20text%20generation%20quality). Just remember that beyond tuning knobs, *creativity also benefits from iteration* – you might need to retry or adjust and not expect the first output to be the best.

## Advanced Strategies: Chain-of-Thought, Role-Play, and Self-Reflection for Creativity

Beyond static prompting and decoding settings, researchers have explored more sophisticated **prompting strategies and workflows** to further enhance creativity. These involve guiding the model through intermediate steps or dialogues, much like how humans brainstorm or refine ideas. Here we discuss a few such strategies – **chain-of-thought prompting, collaborative role-play (multi-agent prompting), and self-reflection loops** – and their effectiveness for creative tasks.

- **Chain-of-Thought vs. “Leap-of-Thought”:** Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting is when you prompt the model to **reason step-by-step**, explaining its thought process before giving a final answer. CoT has proven very powerful for arithmetic and logic problems. One might wonder if guiding an LLM to think stepwise could also yield more creative answers (e.g., systematically exploring ideas). In practice, however, standard CoT tends to **reinforce logical, convergent thinking rather than divergent creativity**. Zhong *et al.* (2024) argue that *“CoT is not conducive to creative problem-solving which often requires out-of-box thoughts”*[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.02439#:~:text=%3E%20Abstract%3AChain,or%20both%2C%20and%20thus%20is). A logical chain can actually constrain the model to typical patterns. For example, if asked to invent a joke, a CoT might have the model analytically explain what a joke is, which could lead to a bland result. Zhong *et al.* instead propose **“Leap-of-Thought (LoT)”**, a paradigm where the model is prompted to make **non-sequential, associative jumps** – more like creative leaps than step-by-step deduction[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.02439#:~:text=for%20logical%20tasks%2C%20CoT%20is,GO%20dataset%20which). They tested this on a creative humor task (the *Oogiri* game, where participants must respond to prompts with clever, unexpected quips)[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.02439#:~:text=paper%2C%20we%20explore%20the%20Leap,Accordingly). Regular LLM behavior failed to produce sufficiently funny or original responses[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.02439#:~:text=game%2C%20we%20first%20build%20a,unrelated%20concepts%20and%20selects%20high), but by training a model with LoT-style instruction (and a self-refinement process), they significantly improved its ability to generate humorous, creative answers[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.02439#:~:text=LoT%20ability%20or%20failures%20of,boosts%20creative%20abilities%20in%20various). The take-away is that **forcing a linear chain of reasoning may hinder creativity**, since creativity often involves skipping ahead or making unusual connections without explicating every step. For developers, a practical adaptation is to prompt *brainstorming in parallel*: e.g., “List some wild ideas (they need not be logical), then choose one.” This keeps some structure (so the model doesn’t just output one idea and stop) but encourages leaps in the brainstorming phase. In summary, use CoT with caution for creative tasks – sometimes a *“think step-by-step” prompt can sanitize the output*. Instead, try prompts like *“Let’s imagine some far-fetched possibilities…”* to stimulate leaps of imagination.
- **Multi-Agent “Discussion” and Role-Play:** Creativity can be amplified by **simulating a group brainstorming session** with LLMs. Instead of a single model generating an answer in one go, we can have multiple instances of the model (or one model assuming multiple roles sequentially) that **bounce ideas off each other**. Lu *et al.* (2024) introduced *LLM Discussion*, a framework where several roles (e.g. a pessimist, an optimist, a realist, etc.) discuss a problem in phases – first generating diverse ideas, then evaluating and converging on the best solution[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2405.06373v1#:~:text=responses%20to%20open,Test%20through%20both%20LLM%20evaluation). By emulating the dynamic of a diverse team, this method produced **more original and well-developed ideas** than a single-pass approach[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2405.06373v1#:~:text=facilitates%20vigorous%20and%20diverging%20idea,frameworks%20across%20various%20creativity%20metrics). They tested it on tasks like the Alternative Uses Test and a Scientific Creativity test, and the multi-LLM discussion outperformed even other multi-turn baselines[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2405.06373v1#:~:text=distinct%20roles%20to%20LLMs%20to,frameworks%20across%20various%20creativity%20metrics). The distinct roles prevented the homogeneity issue (all ideas coming out similar) because each role injected a different perspective. **How to implement this?** One way is through prompt engineering: have the model **role-play a debate**. For example, prompt: *“You are a panel of three experts (a visionary, a critic, and a practical thinker) brainstorming movie ideas. First, the visionary suggests a crazy idea, then the critic finds faults, then the practical thinker refines it.”* By structuring the conversation in one prompt or via the chat API, the model will generate a multi-turn exchange. Another approach is to actually run separate model instances in parallel and feed their outputs to each other in turns (this gives more control). The critical analysis of this strategy is that it **significantly increases computational overhead** – it’s essentially making the model do more work – but it can pay off in creativity. It also can sometimes produce *very lengthy* dialogues which need parsing to extract the final answer. Nonetheless, for high-stakes creative tasks (design brainstorming, complex problem solving), this multi-agent technique mirrors the human practice of team ideation and has shown quantitatively better results on creativity metrics[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2405.06373v1#:~:text=facilitates%20vigorous%20and%20diverging%20idea,frameworks%20across%20various%20creativity%20metrics). It’s a promising direction to get the *most* out of current models.
- **Self-Critique and Iterative Refinement:** Another strategy is to have the model **reflect on and improve its own output**. After the model gives an initial answer, you then prompt it (or another model) to critique that answer or suggest improvements, and feed this back in for a revision. This leverages the model’s strengths in evaluation and generation together. For example: *“Draft an original poem about the ocean. Now, critique this poem’s creativity and suggest one change to make it more original. [Model does so] Please apply that change and give the final poem.”* This process can loop multiple times (generate -> assess -> refine). Researchers call this *self-reflection or self-refine prompting*. Importantly, **this does not require training a new model** – it’s done at inference time with clever prompting. Xu *et al.* (2023) introduced **Self-Refine**, showing that an LLM can iteratively improve outputs in various tasks by incorporating its own feedbackarxiv.org. While their paper focused on correctness and fluency, the same idea applies to creativity: ask the model if the answer is mundane and how to jazz it up. Anecdotally, this works because the model often *can recognize* dullness vs originality in text (even if it doesn’t always produce the most original text in one shot). In fact, one study found that an LLM-based creativity evaluator aligned fairly well with human judgments when steered properly[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2412.06060v1#:~:text=We%20construct%20an%20estimator%20to,judgment%2C%20unlike%20simple%20prompting%20baselines). However, a caution: another finding was that models are *poor self-judges by default* – e.g. a smaller Llama model was bad at judging which of two outputs is more creative[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2412.06060v1#:~:text=similarity%20to%20the%20creative%20attribute,8B%20model%20to%20be%20a). So the prompt must be explicit and perhaps use a stronger model for evaluation (GPT-4 is better at judging text than a smaller model). Also, **repeated self-refinement can converge to a safe middle ground** if not careful – the model might keep smoothing out “flaws” and inadvertently remove some imaginative spark. There is even evidence that self-refinement can amplify a model’s own biases (if a model prefers a certain style, it may self-reinforce that)[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2412.06060v1#:~:text=Steering%20Large%20Language%20Models%20to,refine%3A). So, while self-critique loops are powerful, one should perhaps stop after a couple iterations or ensure diversity in feedback. Despite these caveats, this strategy is useful: it’s like having the model put on an editor’s hat after being a writer, catching opportunities for more novelty.
- **“Chain-of-Thought” for Idea Generation:** A variant of CoT prompting specifically adapted for creativity is to use a **thought chain that diverges**. For example, a prompt might say: *“Think step by step. First, list any wild associations that come to mind about this topic. Next, imagine an unusual scenario using one of those associations. Finally, describe that scenario as a short story.”* This is a structured prompt that still allows divergent thinking in the early steps (the model might list bizarre associations freely) and then guides it to build on them. While not formally studied like LoT, such heuristic prompting can yield excellent results – essentially, you’re giving the model permission to be weird in step 1, then giving it focus in step 2. This approach tries to get the best of both worlds: creativity in the brainstorm and coherence in the final output.

In summary, advanced prompting strategies treat the LLM **not just as a one-shot oracle, but as a process**: brainstorming, discussing, critiquing, and refining. These approaches take inspiration from human creative processes (we often ideate, then filter or workshop ideas). The scientific findings are encouraging – e.g., multi-role discussions clearly boost creativity metrics[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2405.06373v1#:~:text=facilitates%20vigorous%20and%20diverging%20idea,frameworks%20across%20various%20creativity%20metrics) – but they also highlight the **inherent limitations** of a single-pass generation. To get more original content, it helps to break the task into sub-tasks or use multiple “brains” (even if they are all the same underlying model). The critical view is that these methods add complexity and might be overkill for simple creative tasks, but for complex or high-value creative work, they can be the difference between a generic output and a truly inventive one.

## Limitations and Challenges in LLM Creativity

Despite impressive progress, there are significant **scientific limitations and challenges** when using LLMs for creative applications. It’s important for AI developers and researchers to understand these failure modes and constraints:

- **Biases and Safe-Mode Constraints:** LLMs learn from human data and thus can carry over biases or cultural stereotypes into their creative outputs. For instance, in storytelling, a model might unknowingly default characters to certain genders or produce culturally biased analogies. Conversely, efforts to **debias and align models (via RLHF)**, while reducing toxic or biased content, may also **dull the creativity**. Mohammadi (2024) found that reinforcement fine-tuning for alignment caused models to **exhibit lower entropy and less diverse outputs**, essentially **“Creativity has left the chat”**[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.05587#:~:text=reduce%20these%20issues%2C%20their%20impact,and%20creativity%20in%20aligned%20models)[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.05587#:~:text=consequences%20of%20RLHF%20on%20the,of%20prompt%20engineering%20in%20harnessing). Aligned Llama-2 models tended to get trapped in more deterministic phrasings (“attractor states”) and showed a measurable drop in linguistic creativity (nearly 9% decrease in a creativity index after RLHF)[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2410.04265v1#:~:text=AI%20as%20Humanity%27s%20Salieri%3A%20Quantifying,N%20%3D%20600). This highlights a trade-off: strongly aligned models (like ChatGPT) are safer and more consistent, but often *less edgy or unexpected* in their responses. They may avoid controversial ideas or wacky humor to stay safe. For users, this means creative tasks that require pushing boundaries might be hindered by the model’s safety guardrails. It’s a delicate balance – we want the model to *not* produce harmful content, but we also don’t want it to become boring. Ongoing work attempts to steer models along a “creativity axis” in latent space without reintroducing unwanted bias[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2412.06060v1#:~:text=We%20apply%20our%20creative%20steering,and%20creative%20than%20baseline%20generations). For now, one practical approach is to choose a model appropriate to the task: e.g., a base model (less aligned) for maximal originality if you can carefully filter its output, or an aligned model for more “controlled” creativity. But one must **carefully consider the consistency–creativity trade-off**[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.05587#:~:text=diversity,of%20prompt%20engineering%20in%20harnessing) for each application.
- **Hallucinations and Truthfulness:** LLM “hallucination” – the tendency to generate false or unfounded information – is well-known. In a creative context (like fiction), making things up isn’t a flaw; it’s the goal. However, hallucination becomes a problem if the task requires *factual* creativity or if the model’s fabrication undermines the value of the output. For example, if asked for a creative solution to a scientific problem, a model might invent an impossible device that sounds superficially plausible but violates physics – more nonsense than creativity. There is evidence that **higher creativity settings (high temp/diversity) correlate with more factual errors and incoherence**[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.00492#:~:text=temperature%20values%20using%20four%20necessary,chance%20via%20changing%20the%20temperature). Essentially, as the model departs from likely (and often factual) statements, it may say anything. This is a challenge when mixing creativity with reliability. One mitigation is to explicitly constrain parts of the output (e.g. “be creative in style but the core facts must be true”) – though the model might or might not follow such instructions reliably. Another approach is a human or tool in the loop: let the model generate freely, but then verify facts and revise. From a scientific standpoint, hallucination is a byproduct of how LLMs work (probabilistic text generation), and it’s not easily eliminated without also eliminating creative divergence. So, **for tasks that need truth plus creativity**, expect to do some validation of the model’s output.
- **Homogeneity and Originality Limits:** Perhaps the most striking limitation found in recent research is the **homogeneity across LLM outputs**. Wenger & Kenett (2023) showed that different models often produce very **similar “creative” responses** to the same prompt, much more so than different humans do[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2501.19361#:~:text=responses%20from%20humans%20and%20a,limited%20set%20of%20%E2%80%9Ccreative%E2%80%9D%20outputs). In their tests, the diversity among a population of LLM answers was significantly lower than the diversity among a population of human answers[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2501.19361#:~:text=responses%20from%20humans%20and%20a,using%20them%20as%20a%20creative). This means many LLMs are drawing from the same pool of common concepts, and they converge on those. For example, ask several LLMs for a creative use of a brick – chances are a lot of them will say “use it as a paperweight or doorstop” (a common idea) whereas humans might each come up with more varied and quirky uses. Even when using various model families, this “creative homogeneity” persisted[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2501.19361#:~:text=responses%20are%20much%20more%20similar,limited%20set%20of%20%E2%80%9Ccreative%E2%80%9D%20outputs). The implication is that current LLMs, despite architectural and training differences, share a largely overlapping knowledge base and likely have learned similar patterns of “creative” responses (often those present in internet text). This homogenization is a **serious concern** if we rely heavily on LLMs for creative content – we risk a kind of monoculture of ideas, where everything starts to sound the same. It also suggests a limitation in the *depth* of their creativity: they might collectively miss entire classes of ideas that humans could think of. Overcoming this might require fundamentally different training data or encouraging more randomness (with the downsides noted earlier). Some research tries to combat homogeneity by injecting diversity (like the role-play discussion method[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2405.06373v1#:~:text=discussions%20with%20participants%20from%20diverse,frameworks%20across%20various%20creativity%20metrics) or via latent space steering[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2412.06060v1#:~:text=We%20apply%20our%20creative%20steering,and%20creative%20than%20baseline%20generations)), but it remains a challenge.
- **Intra-Model Variability and Reliability:** Another limitation is the **unpredictability** of a model’s creative performance on each attempt. As noted, LLMs can one moment produce a brilliantly original paragraph and the next moment a dull cliché, even with the same prompt. Haase *et al.* (2025) documented this large *intra-model variability*: *“the same LLM, given the same prompt, can produce outputs ranging from below-average to original”*[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.12320#:~:text=only%200.28%25%20of%20LLM,model%20selection%2C%20prompt%20design%2C%20and). This variability is due to the stochastic nature of decoding. For users, it means **consistency is low** – you might have to generate multiple outputs to get one that meets a high creativity bar. In practical deployment, this is tricky: you either accept the variable quality or implement a system of multiple tries and ranking. Additionally, if you need to *measure* a model’s creative ability, a single sample is not reliable; you need averages over many runs (as the researchers did). The risk is **misjudging the model** – one could see an excellent output and think the model is uniformly great, or see a bad one and think it’s poor, when in reality there’s a wide distribution[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.12320#:~:text=variability%3A%20the%20same%20LLM%2C%20given,tools%20in%20creative%20contexts). This ties into evaluation difficulty: creativity is already hard to score, and variance makes it harder.
- **Tendency Toward Cliché and Safe Answers:** LLMs are statistically trained, so the **most probable “creative” content in their distribution might actually be clichés** – things that are repeated often in training data (e.g. formulaic plot twists, common metaphors). Chakrabarty *et al.* (2023) observed that LLM-generated stories often **resort to clichés and overly predictable endings**[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2409.16605#:~:text=creativity,of%20widely%20accepted%20standards%20for). For example, a horror story might *always* end with “...and it was all a dream” or a moralistic conclusion, because those appear frequently in stories the model read. In an AUT prompt, models often give the same few popular alternative uses for objects. This is a fundamental limitation: **LLMs lack true imagination** – they create by remixing known patterns, so truly unconventional ideas (that aren’t a remix of known elements) are rare. They don’t have a conscious avoidance of cliché the way human creators do. Some very recent models attempt to break this by incorporating random knowledge graph hops or other mechanisms to spur less direct associations, but it’s an open problem. Users should be aware that without intervention, a model’s first idea might be the *most* obvious creative idea (the one many people would think of). To get more original outputs, one may need to push the model (via prompt or iterative refinement) to go beyond the first solution.
- **Evaluation Challenges:** Finally, it’s worth noting that even **identifying creative success or failure is hard**. We have metrics like originality or human ratings, but these can be subjective. What one evaluator finds highly creative, another might find irrelevant. This makes it challenging to systematically improve LLM creativity – you need a reliable reward signal or metric. Some automated metrics (e.g. based on semantic distance or large-scale reference comparisons) are in development[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2504.15784v1#:~:text=annotations%20or%20fail%20to%20align,75%20%28%2B15)[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2409.16605#:~:text=The%20evaluation%20benchmarks%20used%20in,primarily%20assess%20semantic%20novelty%2C%20which), but there’s not yet a single agreed benchmark. This means some limitations might be under- or over-stated simply because of how we measure. For example, an LLM might consistently generate slightly boring but sensible ideas, which score moderately on all metrics, versus a model that sometimes produces genius and sometimes nonsense – how do we compare their “creativity”? The field is actively discussing these issues[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2501.19361#:~:text=Should%20we%20run%20human%20creativity,will%20inevitably%20continue%20this%20debate). As a practical matter, developers should use a combination of automated and human-in-the-loop evaluation when pushing the creativity frontier, to truly understand the model’s performance and pitfalls.

**In conclusion**, large language models have demonstrated remarkable but *bounded* creativity. They can surprise us with original combinations and even occasionally outshine average human creativity[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.12320#:~:text=%28AUT%29,of%20human%20creativity), yet they remain fundamentally limited by their training data and probabilistic nature. Issues like output homogeneity[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2501.19361#:~:text=responses%20from%20humans%20and%20a,limited%20set%20of%20%E2%80%9Ccreative%E2%80%9D%20outputs), reduced creativity from alignment[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.05587#:~:text=reduce%20these%20issues%2C%20their%20impact,and%20creativity%20in%20aligned%20models), and the tendency toward safe or known ideas mean that **human oversight and inspiration are still vital** in creative applications. LLMs are powerful creative partners, but not replacements for human creativity. Being aware of these limitations allows us to better use LLMs (e.g. mitigate bias, prompt for more diversity, use multiple models, etc.) and also guides researchers on where to focus next – for instance, developing techniques to inject true novelty or reduce model-wide homogenization. The synergy of human and machine creativity, each overcoming the other’s limits, is likely the path forward in the near term.
