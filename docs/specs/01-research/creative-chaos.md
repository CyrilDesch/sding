# Creative Chaos – How to Find the Next Big Idea

> **Role in the project:** Foundational philosophical inspiration. This document establishes why a chaotic, uncertain environment is *necessary* to produce disruptive ideas rather than incremental improvements — the very context in which Sding was conceived.
>
> **Leads to:** [Automated Solution Concept](../03-vision/automated-solution-concept.md)

---

The notion comes primarily from theories of innovation, popularised notably by the Austrian economist Joseph Schumpeter in the 20th century through his concept of "creative destruction." Schumpeter argued that innovation does not arise from the progressive refinement of what already exists, but rather from a rupture or disruption that generates a state of "chaos" from which new and radically innovative solutions emerge.

This approach was also adopted and popularised in the Silicon Valley startup world, notably by entrepreneurs and investors such as Steve Blank, Eric Ries, and Peter Thiel. They argue that it is precisely in uncertain, chaotic, or even dysfunctional environments that entrepreneurs are pushed to question existing assumptions, experiment rapidly, and innovate radically.

The principle behind this philosophy is simple:

- A **too-stable environment** favours marginal improvement (incremental innovation).
- A **chaotic or uncertain environment** pushes actors to fundamentally reinvent models, thereby generating disruptive or radical innovations.

This is therefore a fairly widespread entrepreneurial vision in the startup world, even if it is not always framed in exactly these terms. This approach encourages entrepreneurs to embrace uncertainty and chaos as opportunities rather than as obstacles.
